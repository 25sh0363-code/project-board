1. Introduction
Alzheimer’s disease is a chronic neurodegenerative disorder that primarily affects memory, thinking, and behavior. It is the most common cause of dementia and leads to progressive cognitive decline. Unlike normal age-related forgetfulness, Alzheimer’s disease interferes with daily functioning and quality of life.

2. Historical Context in India
Although the term “Alzheimer’s disease” was coined in 1906 by German neurologist Alois Alzheimer, historical Indian medical literature contained descriptions of cognitive decline in older adults. Ancient Ayurvedic texts spoke of memory deterioration as part of ageing, linked to imbalances in body and mind. However, Alzheimer’s disease was not widely recognized as a clinical condition in India until the late 20th century.

For many decades, memory loss in the elderly was attributed to normal ageing. Limited awareness, cultural stigma, and lack of diagnostic infrastructure contributed to under-reporting. After the 1990s, with rising life expectancy and increasing focus on non-communicable diseases, Alzheimer’s disease began to be studied and documented more systematically.

In recent years, government initiatives such as the National Programme for Health Care of the Elderly (NPHCE) and the National Mental Health Programme (NMHP) have included dementia care and caregiver support. Increasing numbers of memory clinics, neurologists, and community awareness campaigns have improved recognition and care for Alzheimer’s disease in India.

3. Symptoms
Alzheimer’s disease manifests gradually and worsens over time. Common symptoms include:

Early Signs

Memory loss (especially recent events)

Difficulty recalling names, dates, or conversations

Cognitive Changes

Difficulty with planning, problem-solving, and decision-making

Confusion about time and place

Difficulty performing familiar tasks

Behavioral and Emotional Changes

Mood swings, anxiety, depression

Social withdrawal

Irritability and changes in personality

Advanced Stages

Loss of speech/communication ability

Inability to recognize loved ones

Difficulty walking and performing daily activities

4. Transmission
Alzheimer’s disease is not infectious and does not spread from person to person. It arises from complex interactions among:

Age: risk increases markedly after 65 years

Genetics: certain genes (e.g., APOE-ε4) raise risk

Brain Changes: buildup of amyloid plaques and tau protein tangles

Metabolic and Vascular Factors: diabetes, hypertension

Lifestyle, diet, cardiovascular health, and head injuries also affect disease risk.

5. Treatment and Management
There is currently no cure for Alzheimer’s disease. Treatment focuses on symptom management, slowing progression, and supporting caregivers. Key approaches include:

Medical Treatment
Cholinesterase inhibitors (e.g., donepezil)

NMDA receptor antagonists (e.g., memantine)
These can help with memory and thinking symptoms in some patients.

Non-Drug Therapies
Cognitive stimulation and rehabilitation

Structured routines for daily activities

Behavioral therapy for anxiety/aggression

Caregiver Support
Family education, social support groups, and respite care are crucial. In India, caregiving is often provided by family members.

6. Current Trends in India
India has one of the fastest growing elderly populations in the world, which directly influences Alzheimer’s disease prevalence. Key trends include:

Rapid Increase in Cases: Prevalence estimates range from millions of individuals living with dementia, most of whom have Alzheimer’s disease.

Urban vs Rural Gap: Urban areas have more diagnostic resources, resulting in higher detection; rural areas lag behind.

Awareness Rising: National campaigns and NGOs are improving public understanding.

Research Growth: Indian research institutions are conducting studies on genetics, lifestyle factors, and dementia care models.

7. Future Trends and Predictions
As India’s demographic transition continues:

Projected Increases
Cases of Alzheimer’s disease are expected to rise significantly due to ageing population.

Technological Advances
AI and digital monitoring for early detection

Telemedicine for remote support and diagnosis

Policy and System Strengthening
Integration of dementia care into primary health systems

Community-based support networks

Training more neurologists and geriatric specialists

8. Challenges in India
Alzheimer’s care in India faces several barriers:

Limited awareness and stigma around cognitive decline

Shortage of specialized healthcare providers

Under-diagnosis and late presentation

Financial burden on families, especially where long-term care is needed

9. Prevention and Risk Reduction
While Alzheimer’s cannot be fully prevented, evidence suggests that some lifestyle measures may lower risk:

Regular physical activity

Mental engagement (learning, social activities)

Healthy diet (e.g., fruits, vegetables, whole grains)

Control of cardiovascular risk factors (diabetes, hypertension)

Avoiding smoking and excessive alcohol
