1. Conceptual Overview
Diabetes mellitus is a complex metabolic disorder defined by chronic hyperglycemia resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both. Insulin is a hormone produced by the pancreas that regulates blood glucose by facilitating cellular uptake. When insulin function fails, glucose accumulates in the bloodstream, leading to long-term damage to organs such as the heart, kidneys, eyes, nerves, and blood vessels.

America has one of the highest diabetes prevalence rates globally, driven by lifestyle factors, obesity epidemic, and healthcare disparities.

2. Historical Evolution in America
Diabetes has been recognized in medical practice for centuries, but modern understanding emerged in the 20th century with the discovery of insulin in 1921. Throughout the mid-20th century, diabetes was relatively uncommon in America.

Since the 1980s, diabetes prevalence has risen dramatically, paralleling increases in obesity, sedentary lifestyles, and processed food consumption. By the early 21st century, diabetes became recognized as a major public health crisis.

The CDC and other organizations have implemented prevention programs, and healthcare advances have improved diabetes management, but prevalence continues to rise.

3. Pathophysiology
Type 2 Diabetes (Most common)
Progressive insulin resistance in muscle and liver
Compensatory insulin overproduction
Eventual pancreatic beta-cell failure
Chronic inflammation and lipid toxicity

Type 1 Diabetes
Autoimmune destruction of insulin-producing cells
Requires lifelong insulin therapy

Gestational Diabetes
Hormonal insulin resistance during pregnancy
Increases future diabetes risk

4. Clinical Manifestations
Early Phase
Mild fatigue
Increased hunger
Subtle post-meal drowsiness

Established Disease
Polyuria (frequent urination)
Polydipsia (excessive thirst)
Unintended weight loss
Blurred vision

Advanced Disease
Numbness and tingling (neuropathy)
Recurrent infections
Sexual dysfunction
Delayed wound healing

5. Non-Communicable Nature
Diabetes does not spread through contact. Risk accumulates due to:
Genetic inheritance
Obesity
Sedentary behavior
Poor diet
Chronic stress

6. Treatment Architecture
Lifestyle Therapy (Foundation)
Balanced diet with portion control
Regular physical activity
Weight management

Pharmacological Treatment
Metformin as first-line therapy
Sulfonylureas, DPP-4 inhibitors, SGLT-2 inhibitors, GLP-1 agonists
Insulin therapy when needed

Monitoring
Blood glucose testing
HbA1c every 3–6 months
Continuous glucose monitoring
