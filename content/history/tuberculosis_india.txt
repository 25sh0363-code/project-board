1. Conceptual Overview
Tuberculosis is a chronic infectious disease caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis, a slow-growing bacterium that primarily affects the lungs but can involve almost any organ. Tuberculosis spreads through airborne particles released when an infected person coughs, speaks, or sneezes. Unlike many infections, Tuberculosis can remain latent, meaning the bacteria stay dormant without causing symptoms, sometimes for years.

India carries one of the world’s largest Tuberculosis burdens due to high population density, socio-economic disparities, undernutrition, and coexisting conditions such as diabetes and HIV.

2. Historical Evolution in India
Tuberculosis has been documented in India since ancient times. Ayurvedic texts referred to the disease as Yakshma, describing chronic cough, fever, wasting, and weakness. Historically, Tuberculosis was often fatal due to lack of treatment.

During the colonial era, overcrowding, poor sanitation, and malnutrition led to widespread transmission. Sanatorium care and public health measures provided limited relief. After independence, India launched the National Tuberculosis Programme (1962), followed by the Revised National Tuberculosis Control Programme, which adopted standardized treatment protocols.

In recent years, India renamed its strategy the National Tuberculosis Elimination Programme, aiming to eliminate Tuberculosis as a public health problem. While progress has been substantial, the disease remains a critical challenge.

3. Biological Mechanism (Pathophysiology)
After inhalation, Mycobacterium tuberculosis reaches the lungs and is engulfed by immune cells. Outcomes include:

Latent Tuberculosis Infection: Bacteria are contained but not eliminated

Active Tuberculosis Disease: Immune failure allows bacterial multiplication

Granuloma formation attempts to wall off bacteria, but immune compromise can lead to reactivation. Drug-resistant strains emerge when treatment is incomplete or inappropriate.

4. Clinical Manifestations
Pulmonary Tuberculosis
Persistent cough (>2–3 weeks)

Fever and night sweats

Weight loss and fatigue

Chest pain and coughing up blood

Extrapulmonary Tuberculosis
Lymph node swelling

Bone and joint pain

Meningitis symptoms

Abdominal pain

5. Transmission Dynamics
Tuberculosis spreads via airborne droplet nuclei. Risk increases with:

Prolonged close contact

Poor ventilation

Crowded living conditions

Tuberculosis does not spread through touch, food, or shared objects.

6. Diagnosis Framework
Sputum microscopy

Molecular tests (e.g., rapid nucleic acid tests)

Chest radiography

Drug-resistance testing

Early diagnosis is essential to interrupt transmission.

7. Treatment Architecture

Drug-Resistant Tuberculosis

Newer drugs and strict monitoring
