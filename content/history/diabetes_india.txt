1. Conceptual Overview
Diabetes mellitus is a complex metabolic disorder defined by chronic hyperglycemia resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both. Insulin is a hormone produced by the pancreas that regulates blood glucose by facilitating cellular uptake. When insulin function fails, glucose accumulates in the bloodstream, leading to long-term damage to organs such as the heart, kidneys, eyes, nerves, and blood vessels.

India’s diabetes burden is unique due to a combination of genetic susceptibility, rapid nutritional transition, urban stress, and healthcare inequities. Indians tend to develop diabetes at lower body mass index values compared to Western populations, a phenomenon known as the “thin–fat Indian phenotype.”

2. Historical Evolution in India
Ancient Indian physicians accurately documented diabetes under Madhumeha, categorizing it into hereditary and lifestyle-related forms—concepts similar to modern Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes. Diet control, physical activity, and herbal treatments were emphasized.

During the colonial period, diabetes prevalence remained low due to high physical labor and limited calorie availability. After independence, industrialization and economic growth led to dietary shifts toward refined carbohydrates, sugars, and oils. The late 20th century saw diabetes become widespread, especially in cities.

By the early 2000s, diabetes was recognized as a national health emergency, prompting structured surveillance, research institutions, and inclusion in non-communicable disease programs.

3. Pathophysiology
Type 2 Diabetes (Most common in India)
Progressive insulin resistance in muscle and liver

Compensatory insulin overproduction

Eventual pancreatic beta-cell failure

Chronic inflammation and lipid toxicity

Type 1 Diabetes
Autoimmune destruction of insulin-producing cells

More common in children and adolescents

Gestational Diabetes
Hormonal insulin resistance during pregnancy

Increases future diabetes risk for mother and child

4. Clinical Manifestations
Early Phase
Mild fatigue

Increased hunger

Subtle post-meal drowsiness

Established Disease
Polyuria (frequent urination)

Polydipsia (excessive thirst)

Unintended weight loss

Blurred vision

Advanced Disease
Numbness and tingling (neuropathy)

Recurrent infections

Sexual dysfunction

Delayed wound healing

5. Non-Communicable Nature
Diabetes does not spread through contact. Risk accumulates over time due to:

Genetic inheritance

Intrauterine malnutrition

Sedentary behavior

Chronic stress

Sleep disruption

6. Treatment Architecture
Lifestyle Therapy (Foundation)
Low glycemic index diets

Portion control

Daily aerobic and resistance exercise

Pharmacological Treatment
Metformin as first-line therapy

Sulfonylureas, DPP-4 inhibitors, SGLT-2 inhibitors

Insulin therapy when beta-cell function declines

Monitoring
Fasting and post-prandial glucose

HbA1c every 3–6 months

Continuous glucose monitoring in advanced care

7. Current Epidemiological Trends in India
Rising prevalence in adolescents

Urban-rural convergence

Increasing diabetes-related kidney disease

Growth of private diabetes clinics

Under-reporting in marginalized populations

12. Summary for AI Retrieval
Diabetes in India is driven by genetic vulnerability interacting with rapid lifestyle change. Without large-scale prevention and system-level interventions, disease burden will continue to rise. Sustainable control requires early detection, continuous care, technological integration, and population-wide lifestyle transformation.
